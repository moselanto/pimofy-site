Why Your Best People Shouldn’t Be Doing Data Entry

By Pimofy Digital · 6 min read · 17 June 2026

It happens in almost every growing company. The person who knows your product best, or manages your most important accounts, or could be closing deals — spends a chunk of every week updating spreadsheets, fixing records, and copying data between systems. It feels efficient because the work gets done. It’s actually one of the most expensive things a team can do.

You pay twice

When a skilled, well-paid person does routine data entry, you pay for it twice. First, you pay their salary rate for work a trained specialist could do at a fraction of the effective cost. Second — and this is the larger bill — you pay in the high-value work they didn’t do while their attention was on the spreadsheet. The deal not advanced, the customer not called, the improvement not shipped.

Economists call this opportunity cost. On a team, it’s the difference between a person who’s busy and a person who’s productive.

The myth of “it’s faster if I just do it”

The most common reason skilled people keep routine work is that handing it off feels slower than doing it. In the moment, it often is. But “I’ll just do it” is a decision to keep paying the expensive version of that task indefinitely. The one-time cost of documenting and delegating a task is almost always smaller than the recurring cost of a senior person owning it forever.

Context-switching makes it worse

Routine work doesn’t just consume the minutes it takes. It fragments the day. Every switch between strategic work and a quick data fix carries a cognitive cost — it takes time to refocus, and deep work rarely survives a calendar full of small interruptions. A team constantly dipping into operational tasks never gets the uninterrupted stretches where the highest-value work actually happens.

What good delegation looks like

Delegating routine work badly — handing it off without standards or oversight — just trades one problem for another. Done well, it has three ingredients:

  • A documented process so the task can be performed consistently by someone other than the original owner.
  • A dedicated owner whose job is this work, not someone fitting it around ten other responsibilities.
  • Quality assurance and reporting so you can trust the output without checking it line by line.
